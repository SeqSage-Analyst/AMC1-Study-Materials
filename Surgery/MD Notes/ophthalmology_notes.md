# Ophthalmology — AMC1 Notes

---

## Red Eye — Quick DD by Pupil

| Pupil | Diagnosis |
|---|---|
| Normal | Keratitis |
| Constricted | Iritis / Uveitis |
| Dilated, fixed, no light reflex | **Acute glaucoma** |
| No photophobia at all | Conjunctivitis (bacterial or viral) |

---

## Leukocoria (White Pupil)

- **DD:** Congenital cataract, **retinoblastoma**
- **1st step:** Refer to ophthalmology

---

## Corneal Abrasion

- **C/P:** Pain, tearing, photophobia, decreased vision
- **Inv:** Fluorescein staining
- **TTT:** Pain relief + antibiotics (chloramphenicol)

---

## Eye Foreign Body

- **1st step:** Copious saline irrigation → remove with moistened cotton bud

**Refer to ophthalmology if:**
- Large foreign body
- Metal foreign body
- Deeply embedded
- Embedded at centre of cornea
- Penetrating eye injury → **urgent referral**

---

## Chemical Burn to the Eye

- **1st step:** Copious saline irrigation (at least **3 L**) → then refer to ophthalmology

---

## Orbital vs Periorbital Cellulitis

- **Most common organism:** Staph

| Feature | Orbital | Periorbital |
|---|---|---|
| Eye movement | **Cannot move eyeball** + diplopia | Normal — no diplopia |
| Inv | **CT** | FBC + blood culture (no CT needed) |
| TTT | Admission + IV ceftriaxone + IV flucloxacillin | See below |

**Periorbital cellulitis TTT:**
| Severity | TTT |
|---|---|
| Mild | Amoxycillin/clavulanate |
| Moderate | Admission + IV flucloxacillin OR ceftriaxone |
| Severe / <5 yrs not Hib immunised | Admission + IV flucloxacillin **and** ceftriaxone |

---

## Strabismus

- Transient strabismus → common up to **4 months** (normal)
- Must be corrected before **7–9 years** to avoid amblyopia
- **1st step:** Refer to ophthalmology

---

## Subconjunctival Haemorrhage

- **Causes:** Trauma, severe cough
- **TTT:** Usually none

---

## Neonatal Conjunctivitis (Ophthalmia Neonatorum)

| Timing | Cause | TTT |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Chemical (silver nitrate) | — |
| 2–5 days | **Gonococcal** | IV ceftriaxone |
| 5–14 days | **Chlamydia** | Refer to ophthalmology + oral erythromycin or azithromycin |

---

## Conjunctivitis (Non-Neonatal)

**Bacterial:** Purulent discharge
- **TTT:** Eye toilet + topical chloramphenicol
- **Exclusion from school:** Until discharge stops

**Allergic:** Bilateral watery discharge + itching/burning + eyelid swelling (often atopic child)
- **TTT:** Antihistamines (oral or topical) + artificial tears

**Distinguishing viral vs allergic:**
- Both → bilateral watery discharge with burning
- **Itching → most likely allergic**
- H/o atopy, eczema, allergic rhinitis, asthma → **allergic**
- Viral → can be unilateral or bilateral
- Pus → **bacterial → chloramphenicol**

## Corneal Ulceration

- **1st step:** Fluorescein staining

## Herpes Simplex / Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus

- **C/P:** **Dendritic ulcer** (key word for both)
- Herpes zoster → rash in trigeminal nerve distribution
- **TTT:** Refer to ophthalmology + **acyclovir**

## Acute Glaucoma

### Closed Angle (Acute)

- **C/P:** Sudden severe **unilateral** pain, halos around lights, **dilated fixed pupil**, photophobia, lacrimation, blurred vision, red eye
- **Inv:** Tonometry (measures IOP)

**TTT:**
- **1st step:** Oral **acetazolamide**
- Other drugs: BB (timolol), alpha agonist, pilocarpine
- **Long-term:** Iridotomy
- ⚡ **Atropine is contraindicated** → induces attack

### Open Angle (Chronic)

- Gradual, **bilateral** loss of peripheral vision → **tunnel vision**
- **Risk factors:** DM, myopia
- **Fundus:** Cupping of optic disc
- **Inv:** Tonometry → raised IOP
- **TTT:** BB (timolol), pilocarpine, prostaglandin analogue
- **Long-term:** **Trabeculectomy**

> ⚡ Long-term TTT: Closed angle → **Iridotomy** | Open angle → **Trabeculectomy**

---

## Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)

- **Cause:** Emboli from ipsilateral carotid artery
- **C/P:** Sudden **painless** unilateral loss of vision
- **Fundoscopy:** **Cherry red spot** *(key word)*

**TTT (1st 90 minutes):**
1. **Breathe into a bag** → ↑CO₂ → ↑perfusion
2. Eye massage (only within first 90 min — may dislodge embolus)
3. High-flow oxygen
4. IV acetazolamide → ↓IOP
5. Urgent referral to ophthalmology

---

## Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)

- **C/P:** Sudden **painless** unilateral loss of vision
- **Fundoscopy:** Swollen, dilated veins + haemorrhage
- **TTT:** Refer to ophthalmology

---

## Retinal Detachment

- **Risk factors:** Myopia, DM, trauma
- **C/P:** Flashes of light *(key word)*, floaters, **curtain coming down**
- **1st step:** Tilt head back
- **TTT:** Laser photocoagulation

**Loss of vision after trauma:**
- Immediately after → vitreous haemorrhage
- Flashes of light OR next day → **retinal detachment**

---

## Amaurosis Fugax

- **Cause:** Emboli from ipsilateral carotid artery → sign of impending stroke
- **C/P:** Sudden painless unilateral loss of vision → **vision returns** after minutes/hours
- **Curtain coming down**
- **Exam:** Murmur over carotid
- **Inv:** **Doppler US for carotid** *(most important investigation)*
- **Drug of choice:** Aspirin

**Amaurosis Fugax vs CRAO:**
| Feature | Amaurosis Fugax | CRAO |
|---|---|---|
| Cause | Carotid emboli | Carotid emboli |
| Vision | **Returns** after minutes/hours | **Permanently lost** |
| Fundoscopy | Normal | Cherry red spot |

---

## Macular Degeneration

- **Most common cause of blindness in elderly**
- **Most important risk factor:** Smoking
- **C/P:** Slowly progressive bilateral loss of **central** vision — **wavy lines** *(key word)*
- Dry type → no treatment
- Wet type → **laser**
- Refer to ophthalmology

---

## Diabetic Retinopathy

- One cause of eye floaters

| Stage | TTT |
|---|---|
| Non-proliferative | Control DM |
| Proliferative | Laser photocoagulation |

**Screening:**
- Every **2 years** for DM
- Every **year** if DM + another risk factor (HTN, hyperlipidaemia)

---

## Cataract

- **Most important risk factor:** Age
- **Others:** DM, smoking, trauma, steroids
- **C/P:** Cannot read well at night; cannot see well in daylight
- **TTT:** Phacoemulsification

---

## DD of Eye Floaters

1. Retinal detachment
2. Trauma → bleeding
3. **DM** ← most common cause
- **C/P:** Black dots moving in front of patient
- **Inv:** Fundoscopy

---

## DD of Pupil Abnormalities

**Constricted pupil:**
| Cause | Antidote/Action |
|---|---|
| Morphine / heroin | Naloxone |
| Organophosphorus | Atropine + oximes |
| Intracranial bleeding | CT (must) |
| Iritis / uveitis | Associated with photophobia |

**Dilated pupil:**
- Amphetamine, ecstasy, cocaine, LSD toxicity

**Dilated AND fixed:**
- Acute glaucoma
- 3rd cranial nerve palsy

---

## Trachoma

- **Organism:** *Chlamydia trachomatis*
- **Most common cause of blindness in Aboriginal children**
- **C/P:** Inflammation and scarring of the eyes
- ⚡ **Best advice:** Wash hands
- **TTT:** Single dose of **oral azithromycin** *(oral — NOT topical)*

**Prophylaxis (contacts):**
1. All household members
2. All Aboriginal community members in epidemic

**How:** Single dose oral azithromycin

**Screening:**
- All Aboriginal adults >40 → every 2 years
- All Aboriginal children → opportunistic screening

---

## Dacryocystitis

- Infection of the lacrimal sac
- **C/P:** Pain, redness, swelling over **inner aspect of lower eyelid**
- **TTT:** Oral flucloxacillin + warm compresses
- If abscess → incision and drainage

---

## Hypopyon

- **Pus in the anterior chamber**
- **Cause:** Post-operative complication (e.g. post-cataract surgery) / uveitis
- **TTT:** Refer to ophthalmology

---

## Hyphema

- **Blood in the anterior chamber**
- **TTT:** Refer to ophthalmology

---

## Episcleritis vs Scleritis

- Both → red eye
- **Scleritis** → painful; episcleritis → less painful
- **TTT:** Topical corticosteroids or oral anti-inflammatory medications

---

## Lens Quick Reference

- **Myopia (short-sighted):** Concave lens
- **Hypermetropia (long-sighted):** Convex lens
- **Pinhole test:** Corrects refractive errors (presbyopia)
