## Skin Infections - Bacterial

### Overview of Bacterial Skin Infections

| Condition           | Definition                                                                     | Cause                                           | Key Features                                                                                                            | Treatment                                                                                          |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Impetigo**        | Superficial bacterial infection of the skin                                    | Staph or Strep                                  | Thin vesicles with honey colored crusting. Usually on face, hands, neck & extremities                                   | Topical antibiotics (mupirocin/bactroban). School exclusion for 24 hours after starting antibiotic |
| **Folliculitis**    | Superficial or deep infection of the hair follicle                             | Staph                                           | Yellow pustules with a hair shaft in the center. Can be Barbae or hot tub related                                       | Good hygiene, topical antibiotics                                                                  |
| **Furuncle (Boil)** | Deep extension of superficial folliculitis into dermis and subcutaneous tissue | Staph                                           | 1-5 cm red/tender nodule which may contain pus                                                                          | Simple lesions: warm compress. Severe infections: drainage & antibiotics                           |
| **Carbuncle**       | Large deep abscess                                                             | Staph (risk factor: DM)                         | Usually on nape and the back                                                                                            | Drainage & antibiotics                                                                             |
| **Cellulitis**      | Acute inflammation of the skin                                                 | Strep pyogenes (most common), followed by staph | Fever, redness, swelling, warmth, & tenderness of affected area within 1-2 days of injury. Complication of wound/trauma | Immediate IV antibiotics and elevation of leg. Antibiotic of choice: IV flucloxacillin             |
| **Erysipelas**      | Cellulitis of the face                                                         | Strep pyogenes                                  | Well defined slightly raised painful lesion                                                                             | Hot bags and antibiotics                                                                           |

### Acne
- **Definition**: Obstruction of sebaceous follicles (oil glands)
- **Types**: Open comedones or closed comedones
- **Location**: Usually on face, chest, back
- **Cause**: Propionibacterium acne
- **Lesion types**: Papules, pustules, nodules and cysts
- **Treatment**:
  - 1st line: Topical retinoid
  - Antibiotics: Doxycycline, Clindamycin
  - Severe cases: Oral Isotretinoin (teratogenic)
  - Note: Doxycycline can cause phototoxicity

## Skin Infections - Fungal/Parasitic

### Fungal Infections Overview

| Condition | Other Names | Cause | Key Features | Treatment |
|-----------|-------------|-------|--------------|-----------|
| **Tinea Pedis** | Athlete's Foot | Fungal | White erosions, bad odor. Diagnosis: examine scraping under microscope | Antifungal cream/powders (miconazole). Keep feet dry |
| **Tinea Cruris** | Jock Itch | Fungal | Red/scaly rash on inner thighs/inguinal creases; occasionally buttock, not scrotum or labia. Severe itching | Topical antifungal creams |
| **Tinea Versicolor** | Pityriasis Versicolor | Malassezia furfur (mycelial form) | Multiple patchy lesions (oval shape with fine scales) either light or brown. Typically on back, neck, chest, shoulders. Macules hypopigmented or hyperpigmented with fine branny scaling. Itching. Recurrence is common | 1st line: Selenium sulphide and Zinc. Systemic therapy: Ketoconazole, fluconazole |
| **Tinea Corporis** | Ring Worm | Fungal | Well defined itchy circular patches with scaly borders | Topical antifungals (Miconazole, ketoconazole) |
| **Toenail Fungus** | Onychomycosis | Fungal | Yellow, thick nails, painful, brittle. More likely in toenails due to dark, moist environment | Oral terbinafine, itraconazole |
| **Tinea Capitis** | Scalp Ringworm | Fungal | Scaly: single or multiple patches with hair loss, white fine grayish scales. Black-dot. Keroin: swelling with follicular pustules. Favus: sulfur crusts | Griseofulvin - MAIN TREATMENT |

### Parasitic Infections

#### Pediculosis (Lice)

- The louse is grayish/black
- Can be found in head, body or pubic hair
- **Symptoms**: Itching
- **Spread by**: Shared hats, towels, combs, bedding, clothing, upholstery & headphones
- **Treatment**: Permethrin, gamma benzene

#### Scabies

- **Organism**: Female sarcoptes scabiei
- **Type**: Very contagious STD
- **Primary lesion**: Burrow
- **Predisposing factors**: Poor hygiene, sexual relations
- **Symptoms**: Intense itchy rash with linear burrows
- **Site**: Between fingers, wrist, breast, genitalia
  - Adults: Back and soles are spared
  - Children: Can occur anywhere
- **Clinical presentation**: Nocturnal itching + family history
- **Treatment**: Treat all family members

### Scabies Treatment Details

- **Kids**: Topical cream permethrin 5%
- **Pregnant**: Permethrin
- Re-treat the patient after 1-2 weeks
- Wash all clothes in hot water
- Treat all family members

### If Patient Still Itching After Treatment:
1. Re-treat
2. Cortisone

## Skin Infections - Viral

### Viral Infections Overview

| Condition                 | Cause                           | Key Features                                                                                                                                        | Treatment                                       |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **Herpes Simplex**        | HSV Type I or II                | Multiple vesicles on erythematous base. May cause fever, lymph node enlargement, burning pain                                                       | Oral acyclovir                                  |
| **Herpes Zoster**         | Varicella zoster (reactivation) | Vesicles on red base in band-like distribution (DERMATOMAL). Painful rash, prickly nerve pain                                                       | Antiviral medications (within 72 hours)         |
| **Verruca (Warts)**       | Human papillomavirus            | Genital warts: Condyloma Accuminata. 65% resolve spontaneously                                                                                      | 1st line: Podophylin resin. 2nd line: Imiquimod |
| **Molluscum Contagiosum** | Pox virus                       | Single or multiple flesh colored, dome shaped papules with central umbilication. Very contagious. School exclusion: NO. No treatment usually needed | Usually none needed                             |

### Herpes Simplex Details

- **Type I**: More common
- **Type II**: More recurrence
- **Sites**:
  - Lips → herpes labialis
  - Finger → herpetic whitlow
  - Genitalia → herpes genitalis
- **May cause**: Fever, lymph node enlargement, burning pain

#### Herpes Encephalitis
- **Site affected**: Temporal lobe
- **Main clinical presentation**: Fever, headache, confusion and seizures
- **LP**: RBCs in the CSF
- **Treatment**: IV acyclovir
- **Note**: Main side effect of IV acyclovir = renal failure → excessive hydration

#### Herpes and Pregnancy
- **Clinical presentation**: Painful ulceration (multiple grouped vesicles on erythematous base)
- **Treatment of choice**: Oral acyclovir
- **Treatment of recurrent HS**: Suppressive dose of acyclovir
- **When to do C-section**: If active lesion → CS is a must
- **Suppressive dose**: To the mother from 36th weeks
- **Herpes and lactation**: No breast feeding as long as active infection is there
- **Note**: How to differentiate primary from recurrent herpes → Serology

#### How to Protect the Partner
- No sex during active flare up (Important)
- Suppressive anti-viral to the patient
- Condom

#### Acyclovir Resistant HSV/HZV
- Treatment: Foscarnet

### Herpes Zoster (Shingles) Details

- **Definition**: Shingles, a reactivation of varicella zoster (Chicken pox) virus
- **Mechanism**: Virus remains in the cells of nerve roots in an inactive state
- **Result**: Vesicles on a red base in a band-like distribution (DERMATOMAL)
- **Symptoms**: Painful rash, prickly nerve pain

#### Complications
- Affection of eye → herpes ophthalmicus
- Post herpetic neuralgia → severe pain

#### Investigation
- Swab from vesicles → Immunofluorescence or DNA PCR
- Tzancksmear of HSV or HZV → multi-segmented giant cells

#### Treatment
- **When**: ONLY within 72 hours after onset of rash
- **Medications**: Oral acyclovir, famciclovir, valacyclovir
- **After 72 hours**: Treat only the pain → post herpetic neuralgia → TCA, Gabapentin OR Pregabalin

### Verruca (Warts) Details

- **Etiology**: Human papillomavirus
- **Genital warts**: Condyloma Accuminata
- **Note**: 65% will resolve spontaneously
- **Serology**: Not recommended
- **Complications**: Cervical cancer
- **Treatment**:
  - 1st line: Podophylin resin
  - 2nd line: Imiquimod
  - Auto-suggestion: spontaneous resolution after placebo treatment
  - Others: laser, electrocautery, cryocautery

#### Condyloma Accuminata and Pregnancy
- Course with pregnancy: Usually increases in size
- Most safe treatment: Cryotherapy
- If recurrent after cryotherapy: Treat after labor (leave it alone)

### Molluscum Contagiosum Details

- **Virus**: Pox virus
- **Features**: Single or multiple flesh colored, dome shaped papules with central umbilication
- **Contagious**: Very contagious – self and others
- **School exclusion**: NOOOOO
- **Treatment**: No treatment is usually needed

## Allergic/Inflammatory Conditions

### Comparison: Dermatitis vs Urticaria vs Psoriasis

| Condition              | Type              | Cause                                  | Key Features                                                                                                                                                                                                                               | Treatment                                                                                                                             |
| ---------------------- | ----------------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Atopic Dermatitis**  | Allergic/Genetic  | Hereditary disorder                    | Hereditary disorder. History of asthma, allergic rhinitis, conjunctivitis + Family history. Usually along cheeks, face, trunk. Most common site in children: flexures (antecubital fossa). Dry and papular rash, scratching makes it worse | Good lotions & rehydration of skin                                                                                                    |
| **Contact Dermatitis** | Allergic/Irritant | Contact with allergen                  | Papular and itchy rash resulting from contact with an allergen. Commonly nickel (cheap jewelry, buckles), soaps, perfumes, cosmetics, poison ivy/oak                                                                                       | Cortisone cream                                                                                                                       |
| **Hives (Urticaria)**  | Allergic          | Histamine release (IgE)                | Well defined wheals (solid elevations with central clearing). Extremely itchy & may result in angioedema                                                                                                                                   | Oral antihistamines (DRUG OF CHOICE) e.g., Cetirizine                                                                                 |
| **Psoriasis**          | Inflammatory      | Unknown (increased epidermal turnover) | Increased epidermal cell turnover & thickening of the epidermis. Thick silvery scales loosely attached. If removed → bleeding spots (Auspitz sign)                                                                                         | 1st line: Local cortisone. 2nd line: Local vit D (Calcipotriol). UV light or high potency corticosteroids. Severe cases: Methotrexate |

### Atopic Dermatitis Details

- **Type**: Hereditary disorder
- **History**: Asthma, allergic rhinitis, conjunctivitis + Family history
- **Location**: Usually along cheeks, face, trunk
- **Most common site in children**: Flexures (antecubital fossa)
- **Features**: Dry and papular rash, scratching makes it worse
- **Treatment**: Good lotions & rehydration of skin

### Contact Dermatitis Details

- **Type**: Papular and itchy rash resulting from contact with an allergen
- **Common causes**: Nickel (cheap jewelry, buckles), soaps, perfumes, cosmetics, poison ivy/oak
- **Treatment**: Cortisone cream
- **Primary**: Occurs in any person even not allergic (e.g., strong alkali)
- **Allergic**: Occurs only in genetically susceptible

### Hives (Urticaria) Details

- **Definition**: Allergic reaction resulting in histamine release (IgE)
- **Features**: Well defined wheals (solid elevations with central clearing)
- **Symptoms**: Extremely itchy & may result in angioedema
- **Treatment**: Oral antihistamines (DRUG OF CHOICE) e.g., Cetirizine

### Angioedema Details

- **Type**: Hereditary or acquired
- **Hereditary**: C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency
- **Test**: RAST TEST
- **Most common drug causing**: ACE inhibitors (can happen at any time)
- **Features**: Swelling of lips OR around the eyes
- **Emergency**: Swelling of larynx → IM adrenaline and intubation

### Psoriasis Details

- **Mechanism**: Increased epidermal cell turnover & thickening of the epidermis
- **Features**: 
  - Thick silvery scales loosely attached
  - If removed → bleeding spots (Auspitz sign)
- **Treatment**:
  - 1st line: Local cortisone
  - 2nd line: Local vit D (Calcipotriol)
  - UV light or high potency corticosteroids
  - Severe cases: Methotrexate

### Eczema Herpeticum

- **History**: Patient with atopic dermatitis now has vesicles on top of it
- **Cause**: Secondary herpes simplex infection
- **Risk**: Life threatening
- **Treatment**: Acyclovir

### Other Inflammatory Conditions

#### Pityriasis Rosea
- **Cause**: HHV 6
- **Features**: 
  - Begins with a large scaly pink patch on chest or back – called "HERALD" or "MOTHER" patch
  - Then forms "Christmas tree" pattern
  - Itching
- **Note**: Frequently confused with ringworm
- **Treatment**: Calamine lotion and topical cortisone

#### Erythema Multiforme
- **Cause**: HSV
- **Clinical presentation**: Target lesion
- **Types**:
  - Minor: Affect skin only, self-limiting
  - Major: Stevens-Johnson syndrome
- **Treatment**: Cortisone, lotion antihistamine
- **SJS treatment**: Admission, hydration

#### Fixed Drug Eruption
- **Causative drugs**: NSAIDs, sulphonamide
- **Features**: Fixed to the drug and the site
- **Most common site**: Lips and genitalia

#### Drug Allergy
- **2 main types**:
  - Immediate allergy or anaphylaxis → type 1 allergy (IgE)
  - Delayed → after 7-10 days (serum sickness)
- **Important**: Time of onset after taking the drug

## Pigment Disorders

### Vitiligo

- **Cause**: Destruction of melanocytes
- **Mechanism**: Autoimmune
- **Associations**: Type 1 DM, Hashimoto's, pernicious anemia
- **Treatment**:
  - Narrow band PUVA
  - Steroid
  - Punch graft
  - Skin colored material (camouflage)

## Hair Disorders

### Alopecia Overview

| Condition | Key Features | Treatment |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| **Telogen Effluvium** | Most common causes: Related to stress (post partum or severe stress). Prognosis: good hair regrowth within 4-6 months | Usually none needed |
| **Alopecia Areata** | + family history. Exclamation mark hair. Association: other autoimmune diseases | 1st line: Intralesional and topical corticosteroid (betamethasone, triamcinolone). Surgery |
| **Androgenic Alopecia** | Begins by frontal and temporal recession | Minoxidil, Spironolactone |
| **Alopecia Totalis** | Total hair loss | - |

## Benign Skin Tumors & Lesions

### Lipoma
- Benign fatty tumor
- Usually no treatment needed

### Dermatofibroma
- **Other name**: Fibrous histiocytoma
- **Type**: Common and benign skin tumor
- **Cause**: Trauma and insect bite
- **Features**: Asymptomatic but itching in some cases
- **Treatment**: Usually NO treatment

### Pyogenic Granuloma
- **Type**: Vascular lesion
- **Risk factors**: Pregnancy, trauma
- **Treatment**: Excision (1st line)

### Seborrheic Keratosis
- **Age**: Old
- **Site**: Head, neck, back, trunk
- **Shape**: Stuck on appearance
- **Malignant transformation**: No
- **Treatment**: Cosmetic Removal

### Seborrheic Dermatitis
- **Shape**: Scaly, flaky, itchy, and red skin
- **Age**: Kids
- **If adult**: Suspect HIV
- **Treatment**: Antifungals, topical corticosteroids

### Rosacea
- **Demographics**: Females
- **Features**: Flushing at face. Increases with emotions and hot drinks
- **Treatment**: Metronidazole

### Oral Leukoplakia
- **Clinical presentation**: Whitish adherent patches
- **Cause**: Smoking
- **Risk**: Premalignant → Cancer tongue
- **DD**: Candida (candida can be easily removed with bleeding spots)
- **Investigation**: Biopsy
- **Treatment**: Stop smoking

## Skin Cancers

### Comparison: Skin Cancers

| Feature              | Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)                                                               | Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)                                                                                            | Melanoma                                                                                             |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Commonality**      | Most common skin cancer. Most common cancer in Australia                                 | Second most common                                                                                                       | Less common but most dangerous                                                                       |
| **Malignancy**       | Locally malignant, no distant spread                                                     | Malignant, can metastasize                                                                                               | Most aggressive, can metastasize                                                                     |
| **Main Risk Factor** | Sunlight                                                                                 | Radiation, sun exposure, burn, chronic skin infections, Actinic keratosis, Keratoacanthoma, Bowen's disease, leukoplakia | Sun exposure                                                                                         |
| **Most Common Site** | Face                                                                                     | Lower lip, tongue                                                                                                        | -                                                                                                    |
| **Appearance**       | Pearly raised edge with surface telangiectasia. May develop central ulcer (rodent ulcer) | Relatively quickly growing keratotic lesion                                                                              | Variable - increased size, pigmentation or depigmentation, ulceration, bleeding, pain or induration  |
| **Biopsy**           | Shave biopsy (2mm margin)                                                                | Excisional biopsy                                                                                                        | Excisional biopsy (2-3 mm safety margin)                                                             |
| **Treatment**        | Surgery with safety margin (1st line). Other options: Radiation, diathermy, cautery      | Surgery is main treatment                                                                                                | Surgery main line in all stages                                                                      |
| **Note**             | If margin + for malignant cells after excision → re-excise                               | Carcinoma of lower lip, tongue, Marjolijn ulcer = SCC                                                                    | If margin contains malignant cells → re-excise. If near eyelid or on face → refer to plastic surgeon |

### Basal Cell Carcinoma Details

- **Most common skin cancer**
- **Most common cancer in Australia**
- **Type**: Locally malignant tumor → no distant spread
- **Main risk factor**: Sunlight
- **Most common site**: Face
- **Most common type**: Pearly raised edge with surface telangiectasia → may develop central ulcer (rodent ulcer)
- **Biopsy**: Shave biopsy (biopsy with 2mm margin)
- **Treatment**:
  - Surgery with safety margin → 1st line
  - Other options: Radiation, diathermy, cautery
  - If margin are + for malignant cells after excision → re-excise

### Rodent Ulcer (BCC)
- Central ulcer type of BCC

### Squamous Cell Carcinoma Details

- **Risk factors**: Radiation, sun exposure, burn, chronic skin infections, Actinic keratosis, Keratoacanthoma, Bowen's disease, leukoplakia
- **Features**: Relatively quickly growing keratotic lesion
- **Investigation**: Excisional biopsy
- **Treatment**: Surgery is main treatment

#### Specific SCC Types
- Carcinoma of lower lip → SCC
- Carcinoma of tongue → SCC
- Carcinoma of Marjolijn ulcer → SCC

### Melanoma Details

#### Criteria of Malignancy
- Increased size, pigmentation or depigmentation
- Ulceration, bleeding, pain or induration

#### Risk Factors
- Previous h/o melanoma (10 RR)
- Melanotic/dysplastic nevi (6-7 RR)
- Previous h/o non melanoma skin cancer
- 1st degree relative (2 RR)
- Fair complexion (pale skin) (2 RR)
- Sun exposure (1.5 RR)

#### Most Important Prognostic Factor: Depth
- **Spread**:
  - Local
  - Lymph nodes
  - Distant → brain and liver → black spots → History of melanoma and now mass in brain CT = metastasis

#### Biopsy
- Excisional biopsy (2-3 mm safety margin)

#### Treatment
- Surgery → main line of treatment in ALL stages
- If margin contains malignant cells → re-excise
- If melanoma near eyelid or on face → refer to plastic surgeon

#### Staging and Treatment
- Stage 0: Wide excision
- Stage I: Excision ± lymph node management
- Stage II: Excision ± lymph node management
- Resectable Stage III: Excision ± lymph node management
- Unresectable Stage III, Stage IV, and Recurrent melanoma:
  - Immunotherapy
  - Chemotherapy
  - Palliative local therapy

#### Important Notes
- Investigation: Excisional biopsy (2 mm safety margin)
- Treatment: Wide excisional biopsy (2 cm safety margin)

#### Prevention
- Best advice: Avoid sun exposure (10 am - 4 pm)
- Advice to patient:
  - Avoid sun exposure, sunglasses
  - Skin examination + photography → only in high-risk patients
  - Self examination

### Dysplastic Nevi
- **Features**: Irregular border in trunk
- **Management**: 
  - If suspicious → excision
  - Follow up, avoid sun exposure, sun block, wear hat or shirt

### Other Pre-Cancerous Lesions

#### Bowen's Disorder
- **Features**: Slowly growing well red demarcated patch/plaque
- **Risk**: May turn into SCC
- **Management**: 
  - Excisional biopsy
  - Surgical removal

#### Actinic Keratosis
- **Main risk factor**: Sun exposure
- **Age**: Elderly
- **Appearance**: Dark or light, tan, pink, red, combination, or same pigment as surrounding skin
- **Risk**: Malignancy → SCC
- **Treatment**: Removal

#### Keratoacanthoma
- **Key word**: Rapid progression
- **Main risk factor**: Sun exposure
- **Type**: Premalignant lesion → SCC
- **Features**: Dome-shaped, symmetrical, surrounded by smooth wall of inflamed skin
- **Treatment**: REMOVAL

### Mohs Surgery
- **Best choice**: For Basal cell carcinoma or SCC of face or around eyes
- **Advantage**: Save the tissues

## Miscellaneous Conditions

### Frostbite

- **Definition**: Freezing of tissue at cold temps
- **Affected areas**: Exposed areas (Toes, feet, fingers, nose, cheeks, ears)
- **Late stages**: Cyanosis, gangrene, edema, no pain
- **Treatment**: Cover with warm compress, re-warming in water 40-42°C

### Striae

- **Definition**: A streak or a linear scar
- **Cause**: Results from rapidly developing tension in the skin
- **Common in**: Pregnancy, rapid weight gain, steroid use
- **Treatment**: L-ascorbic acid, isotretinoin, laser

### Candidal Infection

- **Risk factors**: DM, HIV, Pregnancy
- **Types**:
  - Intertrigo: At axilla or under breast
  - Napkin: Erythema, satellite lesions
  - Oral thrush: Whitish lesion that can be easily removed
  - Vaginal: Thick white discharge
- **Treatment**: Topical nystatin, oral fluconazole

### Erythema Toxicum

- **Type**: Newly baby rash
- **Features**: Red rash with tiny white or yellowish papules or bumps
- **Treatment**: No treatment needed

## Important Study Notes

### Melanoma - Key Points
- Investigation: Excisional biopsy (2 mm safety margin)
- Treatment: Wide excisional biopsy (2 cm safety margin)
- Most important prognostic factor: Depth
- Prevention: Avoid sun exposure (10 am - 4 pm)

### Scabies - Key Points
- Very contagious STD
- Treat all family members
- If still itching after treatment: Re-treat → then cortisone

### Herpes - Key Points
- Herpes encephalitis: Temporal lobe affected → RBCs in CSF → IV acyclovir
- Acyclovir side effect: Renal failure → excessive hydration
- Pregnancy: If active lesion → C-section is must
- Differentiate primary from recurrent: Serology
- Resistant cases: Foscarnet

### Skin Cancer Biopsy
- BCC: Shave biopsy (2mm margin)
- SCC: Excisional biopsy
- Melanoma: Excisional biopsy (2-3mm margin)

### Treatment Timing
- Herpes Zoster: Within 72 hours only
- If after 72 hours: Treat pain only (post herpetic neuralgia)
